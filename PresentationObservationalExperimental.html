Experiments and Observational Studies

Intro Stats (De Veaux, Velleman, Bock) - Chapter 13

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you should be able to:

  1. Differentiate between Observational Studies and Experiments.
  2. Identify the components of an experiment: Factors, Levels, and Treatments.
  3. Apply the four principles of experimental design.
  4. Recognize confounding variables and explain how randomization resolves them.

1. Observational Studies

Definition: Researchers observe choices or outcomes without assigning treatments.

  • Retrospective: Looking at past data (e.g., examining hospital records of jeepney drivers for respiratory issues).
  • Prospective: Following subjects into the future (e.g., tracking a cohort of BPO workers for 5 years to see health effects of night shifts).

Example: “Does eating ‘Unli-Rice’ cause diabetes?” If we just survey people, we can only find an association, not a cause.

2. Experimental Terminology

To prove causation, we need an Experiment.

  • Response Variable: What we measure (e.g., Taste Rating).
  • Factors: The explanatory variables we manipulate (e.g., Soy Sauce Brand, Vinegar Type).
  • Levels: The specific values chosen.
    • Soy Sauce Levels: Silver Swan, Datu Puti
    • Vinegar Levels: Cane, Coconut (Tuba)

Defining Treatments

A Treatment is a specific combination of the levels of each factor.

3. The Four Principles of Design

  1. Control: Make conditions as similar as possible for all groups. Keep conditions the same (e.g., same amount of garlic, same cooking time, same pot type).
  2. Randomize: Equalize the effects of unknown variation. E.g. Randomly assign which judge tastes which Adobo to average out bias (e.g., judge’s mood).
  3. Replicate: Use enough subjects so differences aren’t just anecdotes. E.g. Use many judges (n=50), not just one “Tita”.
  4. Block: Group similar subjects together before randomizing. E.g. Group judges by preference first. (e.g., “Visayans” vs “Tagalogs” might have different taste preferences, so we block by region).

Diagrams & Blinding

Diagrams: Visual maps of the experiment structure.

Blinding: Hiding the treatment assignment.

  • Single-blind: The taste-tester doesn’t know which soy sauce was used.
  • Double-blind: Neither the taste-tester nor the person handing them the bowl knows which is which. (Prevents the server from giving “hints” like smiling when serving their favorite brand).

4. Confounding

The Enemy of Inference.

When the levels of one factor are associated with the levels of another factor, we cannot separate their effects.

  • Example: Testing a new teaching method only in the morning. Is it the method or the time of day?
  • Randomization is the cure for confounding.

Another Example

“Board Exam Review Centers”

Students who enroll in expensive Review Centers have higher passing rates. * Is it the center? Or is it because those students have more money (better nutrition/books) and more free time (don’t need to work)? * Socio-economic status is confounding the result.

Summary (Review of Objectives)

  1. Obs vs. Exp: Only Experiments can prove causation; Observational studies only show trends. Observation is like a survey (Unli-rice usage); Experiment is an intervention (Adobo cooking test).
  2. Components: Remember that Treatments are the specific combinations of Factor Levels. E.g. Treatments are the specific recipes (Factor Combinations).
  3. The Big Four: Valid experiments must Control, Randomize, Replicate, and Block. E.g. Control (ingredients), Randomize (tasters), Replicate (sample size), Block (region).
  4. Confounding: We Randomize to ensure that confounding variables are spread equally across groups. E.g. without randomization, other factors (like wealth in the Review Center example) confuse our results.
